WORK AND THE ANARCHIST CRITIQUE OF CAPITALISM

Clara: You may be wondering why this podcast is called “the Ex-Worker.” It’s not that we don’t have jobs—much of this episode was written by renegade workers stealing time on the clock to scribble notes, send emails, and pore over books and websites while their bosses weren’t looking. And it’s not that we don’t need to work because we all have trust funds to squander on a carefree revolutionary lifestyle. On the contrary! If you do have a trust fund and you’d like to spend it on revolution, please report for duty immediately.

No, we call this show the Ex-Worker because we don’t define ourselves in terms of our roles in the economy. We know our lives could be so much more if we could shed the baggage of wage labor and start using our time and energy and resources for the things that really matter. The Ex-Worker springs from our desire to undermine the world of work rather than scrambling to find a better place within it. The CrimethInc. Ex-Workers Collective comprises various projects and individuals who are committed to this vision. And that’s why we decided to focus this episode on Work and the Anarchist Critique of Capitalism.

But the topic is so huge! Where do we start?

While on the run from police in 1897, the Italian anarchist Errico Malatesta started writing a book called “At the Cafe: Conversations on Anarchism.” It presents a basic introduction to anarchist ideas in the format of a bunch of people hanging around in a cafe talking about their lives and their society. We wanted to find a way to communicate our critiques of work and capitalism that reflects how we actually experience them in our daily lives. So with a nod to Malatesta, we present our ideas about the economy in the form of a discussion between three friends hanging out at the coffee shop where one of them is on break from their shift.

We write from the perspective closest to our daily reality – as precarious workers in crappy service jobs in the US. But the dynamics we talk about stretch throughout the pyramid of the global economy. Perhaps you’re listening to this podcast on your commute to your high-powered corporate job. Or maybe you’re streaming it on the computer in the public library where you hang out all day cause you’re homeless. Certainly, capitalism has produced wealth and material comfort for some, while inflicting previously unimaginable poverty on others. But ultimately, we don’t think this system is in anyone’s best interest, when none of us get to choose our activities based on the values that matter most to us rather than what is profitable or marketable. Not to mention how the destruction of the environment is beginning to threaten life on earth! No matter where you’re positioned in the economy – or where you’ll find yourself next month, or next year – we think that you can understand what’s going on and find ways to fight against it.

We’ll return to the café in future episodes to talk about other topics that come up – alternative economies, the difference between anarchism and libertarianism, and the past, present, and future of anti-capitalist resistance. For today, our friends will focus on work: what it is, what it does, who it is and isn’t good for, and how to imagine a world beyond it.

With that, let’s head to the café.

AT THE CAFE: WORK

Friend: Alright, I’m going on break now. Hey y’all.

Clara: Hey.

Alanis: Hey you. How’s the work day treating you?

Friend: You know, it sucks. But what can you do?

Clara: C’mon, I know you don’t want to sling coffee to yuppies for the rest of your life, any more than I want to waste my nights restocking groceries just to rent a room and eat the occasional burrito.

Friend: Of course not. But what else am I going to do? I mean, I could go back to school and get another degree. But then I’d just be in more debt, and there’s hardly any jobs anyway. At least like this, I get to see you on my break.

Alanis: What did you want to do before you got stuck in the endless loop of service jobs?

Friend: I… um… christ, I don’t even know. I mean, there’s stuff I’m interested in, places I’d like to visit or whatever. But in this context it’s hard for me to see much further past getting wasted this weekend or vegging out with Netflix. It’s kind of sad, I guess. But we should be grateful for what we have, right? I mean, at least we have jobs –

Clara: For now…

Friend: – right. And annoying as they are, we’re better off than the homeless guys out there, or the ones who picked the coffee beans we’re drinking, or made the shoes I’m wearing.

Alanis: Sure. But is that our only choice? Being miserable, or being broke and miserable? I know we’re not the only ones who feel this way. Who doesn’t wish they could spend their time the way they wanted to? If you ask me, the problem is work itself.

Friend: Wait, are you serious? How can you be against work?

Clara: You’re a worker. How can you be for it?

Friend: I’m not for it, but everybody has to make a living! I don’t want to spend my life in this coffee shop, but I have to pay the rent and feed my cat. I work to survive.

Alanis: So by work, you mean giving up your time, skill, energy, and so on in exchange for a wage?

Friend: Yeah, I get it, I’m a wage laborer.

Alanis: And that’s how the coffee shop turns a profit. Your work adds value to the owner’s product, and where his profit comes from is the extra amount of value you generate that he doesn’t pay you for.

Friend: So? I make lattes, my boss gives me money so I can pay my rent, and he puts the extra money I made for him back into the business so that I still have a source of income to pay my rent next month, too. That’s how I have some security in my life.

Alanis: I’m not sure how secure that is. Let’s say you never get hurt or poisoned or sick here, and that the economy doesn’t crash and take your savings with it, and that you don’t get mugged by someone who’s worse off than you.

You still can’t be sure you won’t be fired. Nowadays nobody works the same job their whole life; you work somewhere a few years until they let you go for someone younger and cheaper, or they outsource your job overseas. When profit drives everything, you’re only as secure as your ability to step on anyone in your way to make a buck. As for the stability of the economy itself, the crisis taught us how secure that is.

Clara: What could provide real security? Maybe being part of a long-term community where people look out for each other and determine their own needs and desires, rather than just being cogs in the market economy. And the main thing that holds us back from doing that is work! When we have to work as much as we do these days, Facebook is the closest thing we have to a community.

Friend: OK, say we didn’t work—how would we provide for ourselves?

Alanis: Without bosses, you mean?

Friend: Sure, whatever. I mean, what’s your alternative?

Alanis: Think about how many people enjoy gardening, fishing, building, cooking, or computer programming just for their own sake. Would you consider that work?

Friend: That’s too idealistic. There’s no way that kind of activity could provide for all our needs, even if there weren’t bosses and landlords.

Alanis: But that’s how it used to be, for most of the time humans have been around. Think about how differently Work has worked for most of our history.

Friend: Yeah, but we’re so much more productive today.

Clara: But what do we produce? Enough waste and useless crap to cover the planet in landfills, while there are still a billion people going hungry. What kind of society creates all this abundance while preventing us from actually enjoying it? Our economy is great at producing commodities, to the extent that the earth is running out of anything we can use, but terrible at producing happiness.

Alanis: And anyway, nowadays in the US at least, many of the jobs don’t produce anything useful: they’re basically pointless, like yours here in the café. In the service sector, we’re basically human advertisements that give our employers a marketing edge. I can pour my own coffee; you’re just here to create an atmosphere that makes people want to buy it. Most work as we know it isn’t necessary at all. Outside of a capitalist economy, it would be worse than useless.

Friend: Well, even if our jobs aren’t productive in any useful way, at least for some people, work can be fulfilling.

Clara: Let me tell a story. My dad worked programming computers all his life; he knew since he was a kid that he loved computers, he went to school for it, and did it every day for decades. But when I was growing up, he’d come home frustrated from work every day and over dinner he’d gripe about his lazy coworkers, his idiot bosses, all the ways the management made his life harder… He was clearly unhappy. But the weird thing was, if you ever asked him outright, he’d always say, “oh yeah, I love my job.”

Friend: Why?

Clara: I never understood that, until I realized that his job turned activity that he loved into work that he hated. If he could have done what he always wanted to do on his own terms, instead of following orders from a company motivated only by profit, he could have found fulfillment. Instead, in order to support our family, he was trapped in this exasperating job that soured him on the thing he used to enjoy most.

Work isn’t what fulfills us- often it turns whatever we do into drudgery, by forcing us to evaluate it in terms of profit rather than how fulfilling it is. Plenty of baristas like you love coffee and can take joy in a perfectly poured cappuccino… which then turns to ashes in their mouths when they hand it over to a cranky yuppie who doesn’t even bother to look at it.

Alanis: Or on the flip side, we measure our achievement by the tips we get for our lattes, our grades or the promotions we earn, instead of how much the things we do enrich our lives and those of the people we care about.

Friend: So, yeah I know. Work isn’t fulfilling for me either! I know that, and I’ll go to work tomorrow, because I have to. But some people chose to go because they get something out of it.

Clara: What do they get, exactly?

Friend: I don’t know. It… it creates initiative, a sense of responsibility…

Clara: What kind of initiative? If everything in our society has to be driven by a profit motive or an external authority to succeed, is that really initiative? What if working actually eats away our initiative? I remember when I dropped out of school, I started working at a diner, and even though it was a crappy job, sometimes on my days off I’d wake up and think, huh, what am I going to do today? And I’d almost wish that I was scheduled to work! The things that actually matter to us get bumped to the bottom of the to-do list, and we learn to only follow through on commitments based on external rewards or punishments, not our passions or values.

Alanis: As for responsibility… Working seems to foster the impression that getting a wage absolves you of responsibility for your actions. Who carried out most of the injustices in history? It was employees, right? You know the Nuremburg defense—“I was just following orders"? Isn’t that what we all tell ourselves? So much of what’s wrong with the world today comes from this willingness to check our consciences at the door when we clock in.

Clara: Example: the people who picked the coffee beans you’re selling were paid poverty wages in terrible conditions. Sucks, right? But it’s not your fault; you’re just the barista. And it’s not your boss’s fault; he can’t control the work conditions in Costa Rica or whatever. And on and on, up the chain- everyone’s got an excuse. And the gears keep grinding on in a system no one can pretend is just. How is that teaching responsibility?

Alanis: But the real problem isn’t employees refusing to take responsibility for their actions—it’s the economic system that makes taking responsibility so expensive.

Friend: But… sigh. Fine, work sucks, but we can’t all just stop working and do nothing!

Clara: Not working doesn’t mean doing nothing. It means doing things because they matter, not because they’re profitable. Think what we could accomplish if we were free to use our time and energy how we wanted to instead of going to work!

Alanis: The bottom line is simple: we all deserve to make the most of our potential as we see fit, to control our own destinies. Being forced to sell these things away just to survive is miserable and tragic.

Clara: We don’t have to live like this. You can-

Boss [interrupting]: Ok, your break’s over.

Boss: We’ve got a long line here. C’mon, time to get moving.

THE CHOPPING BLOCK

Clara: Now it’s time for the Chopping Block, where we review something that anarchists are reading and tell you what we think. This episode we’re taking a look at Silvia Federici’s book Caliban and the Witch: Women, the Body, and Primitive Accumulation.

Alanis: In “Caliban and the Witch”, Italian feminist Silvia Federici traces the historical developments that laid the groundwork for the emergence of capitalism in Europe. Building upon and critiquing earlier radical histories of the period and the theories of Karl Marx and Michel Foucault, she examines heretic movements, struggles over reproduction, and the witch-hunts through a feminist lens. Her analysis focuses on Marx’s concept of primitive accumulation, the initial violent theft of resources and labor that served as the foundation of capitalism. She expands this notion to focus not only on the initial appropriation of land, gold and slaves that made the production of commodities possible, but the ongoing expropriation of women’s bodies and labor required for the reproduction of the workforce. In medieval Europe, she argues, this process was catalyzed by the mass extermination of women deemed witches and the destruction of radical peasant and heretic movements that challenged feudal power relations. Capitalism was the counter-revolution of the powerful against the threat posed by these alternate modes of organization.

“Caliban” was published in 2004 by Autonomedia, a New York-based collective focused on radical and especially anarchist theory. It appeared at the tail end of the anti-globalization era, a time of reflection among anarchists and autonomists about the global movement they’d influenced and the future of the neoliberal capitalist system they’d fought. Federici wisely observes that neoliberal institutions such as free trade agreements and the IMF and World Bank, which sparked mass demonstrations and riots on four continents, represent a new wave of primitive accumulation. So understanding how this accumulation happened in medieval Europe as feudalism gave way to capitalism provides directly relevant lessons for our struggles today. This helps explain the book’s recent renewed popularity, particularly among anarchists seeking to understand the new primitive accumulation in our own age of austerity, economic recession, and state repression.

This popularity also reflects the strategic application of certain Marxist concepts within a larger anarchist framework; Federici engages with them while challenging Marx’s linear view of history and limited analysis of gender and reproductive labor. Though Federici is not an anarchist herself, her politics are rooted in the autonomous feminism of 1970s Italy, which continues to influence contemporary anarchism; for example, both urban squatting and property destruction emerged as widespread militant tactics from this time period and trajectory.

The book’s tone can be a little dry or theoretical at times, but on the whole it’s quite engaging, full of rebellious peasants and defiant witches amidst insightful analysis of struggles then and now. It leaves us wanting to know more about the processes of primitive accumulation and witch hunting in the New World, and how to apply her analysis to our North American context. As it stands, “Caliban and the Witch” offers a crucial analysis of gender and economic history for the well-informed radical. Whether you’re interested in feminist challenges to traditional radical theory, fascinating histories of medieval rebellion, or lessons for anti-capitalist struggle today, we highly recommend it.

MUGSHOTS

Clara: And now we bring you this episode’s Mugshot, the feature in which we profile a group or project that’s putting anarchist ideas into action today. A couple weeks ago correspondents from the Ex-Worker had the opportunity to attend a conference in Waterloo, Ontario called (En)gendering Resistance, which focused on themes of gender, militancy, and resistance through a largely anarchist lens. So in this episode we’re going to speak with Tammy, one of the conference organizers, about what happened, the group that pulled it together, and the context of anarchist organizing in that region of Canada.

Engendering Resistance was organized by the Waterloo Public Interest Research Group (WPIRG), a group based at the University of Waterloo that provides a place to research, educate, and take action on environmental and social justice issues. They’re funded by student fees but operate with a fair bit of autonomy; so despite a fairly liberal sounding mission, many anarchists participate and are able to have the resources to do interesting work.

Tammy: Basically we act as a radical hub on campus to provide students with the opportunity to take action on things that they’re interested in. We have a radical lending library, we have groups that work on issues like prison abolition, indigenous solidarity work, work around gender and addressing sexual violence in our communities, anti-poverty organizing and the like.

Clara: Waterloo is a university town of just under 100,000 people in southern Ontario, northwest of the US border at Buffalo. The nearby cities of Guelph, Hamilton, and Toronto have active radical communities, many of whom participated in the conference. Tammy discussed the context for anarchist organizing in the region over the past few years, framed by the mass mobilization three years ago against the G20 summit:

Tammy: In 2010, the G20 happened in Toronto; anarchist networks in the region were very heavily infiltrated and targeted; there were large conspiracy cases, a lot of repression, people facing charges. So you had a whole bunch of organizers removed facing legal battles, those not facing legal battles putting a lot of time into supporting those that were, and that really affected the level of organizing. That’s all wrapped up, and things I think are bouncing back. There’s a lot of organizing that’s started off recently around the Line 9 reversal in Ontario, so Tar Sands oil will be going through Ontario soon through a pipeline that’s already there. Anarchists have been getting really involved in that. And there’s a lot of anarchist involvement in the region, a lot around prison support, and just the general anarchist organizing: book fairs, infoshops, things like that.

Clara: One of the main projects WPIRG undertakes is organizing an annual conference for radicals in the region. Each year the organizing collective chooses a different theme: past conferences had focused on topics like prison abolition or food justice. Tammy explained why they decided to focus this year on gender and militancy:

Tammy: On the one hand, organizers in the collective felt really frustrated with the sort of direction and discourse some of the more mainstream feminist groups in the area use, or sort of their analysis and wanted to intervene and provide a space to facilitate discussions that put forth the idea that a meaningful engagement with questions related to gender liberation must also include things for instance like critique of the state and a critique of capitalism and white supremacy, and sort of broadening the discussions of gender as we saw them playing out. You have, for instance, a lot of groups in our area might do great work addressing certain gender issues, but work a lot with the regional police and don’t have any type of analysis of being critical of state forces.

The flip side for that, as people who work in a lot of other spaces, whether it’s anarchist spaces or more broad anti-capitalist spaces, being frustrated seeing discussions of gender sort of put to the side; folks have a great analysis of state repression or have a great analysis of capitalism, but leaving out or undervaluing the discussion of working in gender to the work that we’re doing.

Clara: Although WPIRG itself isn’t explicitly anarchist, the organizers felt it was crucial to ground the content, organizing process, and structure of the conference in anarchist values.

Tammy: In terms of content for the conference, there was an open call-out for proposals, but we definitely prioritized proposals that engaged with questions of gender from a more radical perspective and put forward solutions that were community-based and not relying on state institutions or parliamentary solutions to gender, and also trying to open up discussions of gender, and wanting to work in discussions of how it relates to anarchism itself.

In terms of the organizing process, it was organized by a non-hierarchical collective; everyone took on responsibility and were actively involved in deciding the theme, what the weekend would look like, taking on different roles. And then in terms of the structure of the weekend itself, an ongoing emphasis was placed on trying to make it as accessible as possible for folks, so taking things into consideration like having it in a physically accessible venue, providing ASL, childcare, having it free, having the conference be free, food provided for the entire weekend, and even trying to pay for facilitators to be able to come in, so that no economic issue would be a barrier to participating in the conference.

Clara: Engendering Resistance took place April 19th–21st on the campus of the University of Waterloo. Over the course of the weekend, some two to three hundred people attended, largely folks actively engaged in resistance movements in the region as well as others from the US and other regions of Canada. There were two full days of workshops on a fascinating range of topics:

Tammy: Community accountability, and critical discussions around addressing sexual violence, the gendering of militancy and different forms of resistance, queering anarchism, beyond the politics of self-care, history of feminist thought and practice, feminism and independent media, femme identity in anarchist communities, creating anti-racist feminist spaces…

Clara: A powerful keynote talk by Jackie Wang discussed “Revolutionary Loneliness,” laying out a theory of alienation through the lens of the experiences of women and gender variant people who took part in 20th century revolutionary struggles.

Tammy: We also hosted a performance, a few performances, film screenings, and the Saturday evening of the conference organized and hosted a noise demo at GVI, which is the Grand Valley Institute for Women, which is a federal prison located in the city. So it was one small attempt to try to ground what we’re doing for the weekend conference not only in discussions, but having a small action component in there.

Looking back on the weekend, Tammy appreciated the relationships built through these conversations while recognizing the limitations of the conference framework.

Tammy: You can have good discussions and bring people together in that way, but in terms of tying it to concrete action or figuring out ways to continue on the conversation after everyone has dispersed and gone back to their different cities is always a challenge and really difficult to measure.

Clara: In the end, what matters is how these events expand our capacity to resist. And Engendering Resistance provided new ideas and connections to further our struggles against gender oppression and all forms of hierarchy.

If you want to learn more about Engendering Resistance or WPIRG, you can find links at our website at crimethinc.com/podcast.
